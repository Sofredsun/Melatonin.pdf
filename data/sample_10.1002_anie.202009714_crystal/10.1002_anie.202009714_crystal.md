## Manipulating Light-Induced Dynamic Macro-Movement and Static Photonic Properties within 1D Isostructural Hydrogen-Bonded Molecular Cocrystals 

Shuzhen Li, Bo Lu, Xiaoyu Fang, and Dongpeng Yan* 

Abstract: Smart molecular crystals with light-driven mechanical responses have received interest owing to their potential uses in molecular machines, artificial muscles, and biomimetics. However, challenges remain in control over both the dynamic photo-mechanical behaviors and static photonic properties of molecular crystals based on the same molecule. Herein, we show the construction of isostructural co-crystals allows their light-induced cracking and jumping behaviors (photosalient effect) to be controlled. Hydrogen-bonded cocrystals from 4-(1-naphthylvinyl)pyridine (NVP) with coformers (tetrafluoro-4-hydroxybenzoic acid (THA) and tetrafluorobenzoic acid (TA)) crystallize as isostructural crystals, but have different static and dynamic photo-mechanical behaviors. These differences are due to alternations in the orientation of NVP and hydrogen-bonding modes of the coformers. After light activation, the 1D NVP-TA crystal splits and shears off within 1 s. For NVP-THA, its photostability and high quantum yield give novel photonic properties, including low optical waveguide loss, highly polarized anisotropy, and efficient up-conversion fluorescence.

## Results and Discussion 

Herein, we report that the dynamic photo-mechanical and the static photonic properties (including optical waveguide, polarization and up-conversion fluorescence) of molecular crystals are effectively tuned on and off using the design of the isostructural co-crystals. 4-(1-Naphthylvinyl)pyridine (NVP, Figure 1) is selected as the target building block owing to its excellent optoelectronic properties; notably, NVP has also been applied in logic gates and optical switches.[[53]] Moreover, the potential [2 + 2] cycloaddition of the olefin pairs in NVP make it possible to achieve a microscopic photo-mechanical response. Tetrafluoro-4-hydroxybenzoic acid (THA) and tetrafluorobenzoic acid (TA) both have similar sizes and shapes, and are attractive hydrogen-bond-organized co-former units for supramolecular assemblies, which facilitate the formation of isostructural crystals and modification of their intermolecular interactions and photonic performances. The different numbers of hydrogen-bond sites in THA and TA may further regulate the process of photo-mechanical conversion. NVP-TA has exhibited rapid photo-induced cracking and movement based on the energy conversion from light to mechanical energy, termed the photosalient effect.[[11,12,15,54,55]] More specifically, the photosalient effect stands for a mechanical response (including a sudden and rapid burst, forceful jumps, and/or spring off many pieces) after exposure to UV light. This effect presents a visualized process through the release of stress during the photochemical reaction. The isostructural NVP-THA has high photostability without lightdriven jumping and shows well-defined, static photonic performances in 1D optical waveguide, polarization and upconversion fluorescence. Co-crystallized NVP-THA and NVP-TA were fabricated through a facile, solution-based process in methanol using a 1:1 stoichiometric ratio under ambient conditions. Within one week, this approach yields two types of one-dimensional (1D) needle-like transparent microcrystals. Single-crystal X- ray diffraction (XRD) measurements show the NVP-THA and NVP-TA crystals both belong to a monoclinic crystal system (space group P21/c) and have nearly identical cell parameters, presenting with the same two-dimensional (2D) lamellar structures through hydrogen bonding and p-p interactions (Figures S1 and S2). Thus, NVP-THA and NVP-TA are regarded as isostructural systems. In both cocrystals, proton transfer occurs from the hydrogen-bond donor (THA and TA) to the acceptor (NVP), thus forming a strong charge-assisted hydrogen bond between two assembled units. The connection of NVP and TA/THA molecules are interacted through hydrogen bonds to form infinite columns (Table S1). Both the NVP and co-former units exhibit anti-parallel arrangement modes, in which the NVP dimers form an H-type aggregation, with distances of 3.55 and 3.61 �between the neighboring olefin groups of the adjacent NVP (Figure S3). This aggregation facilitates the [2 + 2] cycloaddition upon UV irradiation. It is also noteworthy that besides the intermolecular hydrogen bonds (e.g., N[�] H···O, C[�] H···O and C[�] H···F) in the NVP-TA co-crystal, the NVPTHA motif also involves another O[�] H···O hydrogen bond between THA molecules (Figure S4). Additionally, powder XRD profiles of NVP-THA and NVP-TA are in agreement with their simulated patterns (Figure S5), suggesting the high yield and purity of the as-prepared samples during cocrystallization. The strong (200) and (020) diffraction peaks in the XRD patterns indicate obvious preferred orientations for both NVP-THA and NVP-TA. 

To determine any potential photo-active properties between two types of isostructural molecular crystals, 1D NVPTA and NVP-THA crystals were first observed under fluorescence microscopy excited by 365 nm UV light (100 mWcm[�][2] ). Interestingly, NVP-TA crystals exhibit an obvious photo-responsive behavior (e.g., splitting or explosion) upon UV excitation. As shown in Figure 2a, the NVPTA crystal retains its integrity and transparent 1D rod-like structure before irradiation, and has a well-defined green photoemission. After UV light irradiation, the NVP-TA crystal starts to crack along the [002] direction obviously, with the fracturing degree increasing with the increasing duration of UVexposure. The green fluorescence at the cracked points also becomes brighter relative to the unbroken locations; this can be a result of the efficient optical waveguiding property in the 1D molecular crystal, which allows fluorescence visualization during the dynamic photo-mechanical conversion process. The fluorescence spectra for NVP-TA also confirm that the emission intensity at 490 nm in the same position is increased greatly after splitting (Figure 2b); however, there is no obvious change in the emission wavelength.

Firstly, the microscopic surface morphology of the single crystal was analyzed using AFM (Figure 3). Before photo-irradiation, the surface of the NVP-TA crystal is very smooth and flat, with an average roughness value of 1.04 nm. As the NVP-THA is the isostructural hydrogen-bonded co-crystal of NVP-TA, it is surprising that no similar photomechanical behavior is observed (Movie S5). The results indicate that the 1D NVP-THA crystal maintains its integrity and crystallinity after irradiation for 360 s. During the photoirradiation, the fluorescence intensity also remains unchanged (Figure S15). The DSC measurements show nearly identical exothermic peaks positions at 172 8 C (Figure S16). Single crystal XRD analysis further confirms that there is no structural change before and after UV irradiation. Thus, the isostructural NVP-TA and NVP-THA co-crystals present completely different behaviors upon light stimulus, with the former showing rapid light-driven mechanical movement and the latter photo-stability. To understand such obvious discrepancies, detailed structural differences and intermolecular interactions within the co-crystals are further analyzed (Figure 4). As is already known, the [2 + 2] cycloaddition reactions in the solid state need to fulfill Schmidt�s geometric criteria,[[61]] i.e., the central distance between the olefin pairs (C[=] C) should be less than 4.2 �, and the relative orientation angles (q1, q2 and q3, Scheme S1) between the olefin pairs (C[=] C) prefer their ideal values (0 8 , 90 8 , and 90 8 respectively). In this work, the distances of separation between the olefin pairs in adjacent NVP molecules are 3.61 �and 3.55 �in the NVPTHA and NVP-TA co-crystals, respectively, which meet the required distance for the cycloaddition reaction to occur after UV irradiation. However, for the angle criteria (Table S5), the q1, q2 and q3 angles are 0.31 8 , 86.43 8 , and 85.74 8 respectively in NVP-TA, which are much closer to their ideal values than those in NVP-THA (0.97 8 , 78.72 8 , and 82.70 8 ). Thus, both the distance and angle criteria are beneficial to the photochemical [2 + 2] cycloaddition reactions for the NVP-TA crystal.

Firstly, the two-photon absorption (TPA) cross section of NVP-TA solution (10[�][5] molL[�][1] ) was detected, and the TPA values are found in the range of 26.9–62.1 GM (1 GM = 10[�][50] cm[4] sphoton[�][1] molecule[�][1] ) from 720 to 780 nm (Figures S20). The excitation-dependent up-conversion fluorescence experiment for NVP-THA were further conducted with the typical excitation wavelengths (740, 760, 780, 800 and 820 nm). Using excitation with a 740 nm laser light as an example, NVP-THA exhibits strong up-conversion fluorescence with maximum emission peaks at approximately 485 nm, which is very close to the position under 365 nm UV light. Due to the centrosymmetric space group, there is a lack of second harmonic generation effect for the co-crystal. Moreover, changing the excitation wavelength ranging from 760 to 820 nm, the shape and position of the emission peak are almost the same as those excited at 740 nm (Figure 5i and Figure S21). Moreover, the fluorescence intensity exhibits a nearly linear relationship as increasing pump powers, with the corresponding slopes of log(intensity) to log(excitation power) as 2.00, 1.92, 1.97, 1.94, 1.86 respectively. This illustrates the two-photon absorption facilitates up-conversion emission, and the photoemission intensity can be finely tuned by controlling the long-wavelength excitation. Therefore, the 1D NVP-THA microcrystal can function as a novel blue two-photon light-emitting material for potential nonlinear optical applications.

Benefitting from their high stability without photo-bleaching, the NVP-THA co-crystals present novel micro/nano-sized photonic properties with high-performance polarized fluorescence, two-photon photoemission, and optical waveguide properties, as a result from strong 1D anisotropy and well-defined crystalline quality. Hence, hydrogen-bond organized isostructural co-crystallization provides a promising strategy to tune photomechanical and photophysical properties of molecular materials through the selection of suitable building blocks. Using this approach would open up a new way to develop other similar actuators and/or photonic materials towards more effective energy conversion.
